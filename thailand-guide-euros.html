Day-by-Day Itinerary

01 Arrival

Arrive Bangkok — Suvarnabhumi Airport

  • Airport Rail Link to Phaya Thai, then BTS Skytrain to hotel (~45 min, THB 45)
  • Buy AIS or DTAC tourist SIM at arrival hall (~THB 299 for 15 GB, 30 days)
  • Evening: Wat Arun lit at night — best photographed from the opposite riverbank
  • Khao San Road for people-watching — chaotic, fun for one evening
  • Or: taxi to Yaowarat (Chinatown) for grilled seafood on Phadungdao Street
Eat: Pad Thai Thip Samai (est. 1966, original Pad Thai — queue is worth it) or Raan Jay Fai for Michelin-starred street food (book ahead, THB 1,000+).
02 Bangkok

Grand Palace, Wat Pho & Chao Phraya

  • Grand Palace complex — Emerald Buddha, Wat Pra Kaew (arrive at 8 AM opening)
  • Wat Pho: reclining Buddha, oldest temple in Bangkok, free to wander
  • Chao Phraya river ferry to Wat Arun (Temple of Dawn) — THB 5 crossing
  • Pak Khlong Talat flower market — thousands of marigolds, best at dusk
  • Chatuchak Weekend Market (Sat/Sun only — world’s largest outdoor market)
Eat: Or Tor Kor Market near Chatuchak — the upscale government fresh market with excellent prepared food stalls. Som Tam Nua for the best green papaya salad in the city.
03 Bangkok

Temples, Art & Modern Bangkok

  • Wat Saket (Golden Mount) — 318 steps, city panorama, quieter than the Grand Palace
  • Jim Thompson House — beautiful Thai silk museum in a canal-side garden
  • Thonglor / Ekkamai neighbourhood — Bangkok’s best dining and bar district
  • Vertigo Rooftop Bar (Banyan Tree Hotel) for sunset over the skyline
  • Fly to Chiang Mai in the evening (DMK → CNX, ~1h 20m)
Eat: Dinner in Yaowarat (Chinatown) — crab fried rice and roast duck at T&T Pochana. Or blow out at Nahm or Le Du (both world-ranked Thai fine dining, book ahead).
04 Travel + CNX

Arrive Chiang Mai — Old City Temples

  • Arrive Chiang Mai Airport — Grab to hotel in or near the Old City moat
  • Wat Phra Singh — the most revered temple in Chiang Mai, serene courtyard
  • Wat Chedi Luang — partially ruined 14th-century chedi, free evening access
  • Three Kings Monument & historic city wall walk
  • Saturday Walking Street (Wualai Rd) or Sunday Walking Street (Tha Phae Gate) if timing aligns
Eat: Khao Soi at Huen Phen or Khao Soi Lung Prakit Kad Kom — the northern curry noodle that makes Chiang Mai worth visiting for food alone. Order it for your very first meal.
05 Chiang Mai

Elephant Nature Park — Full Day

  • Full-day visit to Elephant Nature Park — ethical rescue sanctuary (book weeks ahead)
  • Feed, walk alongside, and learn the history of each rescued elephant
  • Mae Wang Valley scenery on the drive back to Chiang Mai
  • Evening: Nimman Road neighbourhood — independent cafes, boutiques, bars
  • Night Bazaar if energy allows — a more tourist-oriented but atmospheric market
Eat: Breakfast and lunch included at ENP. Evening: SP Chicken on Nimman for famous rotisserie chicken — order a half bird with sticky rice and papaya salad. Queue expected.
06 Chiang Mai

Doi Suthep, Mountain Villages & Fly South

  • Wat Phra That Doi Suthep — golden chedi on the mountainside, city views at sunrise
  • Doi Inthanon National Park — Thailand’s highest peak, twin royal chedis in forest
  • Hmong hill tribe village visit near the Doi Inthanon summit
  • Return to Chiang Mai, evening flight to Krabi (CNX → KBV, ~1h 40m)
  • Check in Ao Nang — the best base for Krabi exploration
Eat: Early market breakfast near the Old City before Doi Suthep. Arrive Krabi for dinner at Carnivore Steak & Grill on Ao Nang beachfront — good Thai-Western seafood.
07 Krabi

Railay Beach, Sea Caves & Rock Climbing

  • Long-tail boat from Ao Nang to Railay Beach (~15 min — only accessible by sea)
  • Phra Nang Cave Beach — sacred princess cave with towering limestone backdrop
  • Rock climbing on the karst cliffs (many operators in Railay East — all levels)
  • 4 Islands snorkelling tour by longtail (book in Ao Nang, full day)
  • Sunset on Railay West beach with a cold Singha
Eat: Seafood BBQ at any Railay beach shack — order grilled tiger prawns by weight. Return to Ao Nang for dinner. Night market along Ao Nang beach road on weekends.
08 Krabi

Tiger Cave Temple, Sea Kayaking & Ferry to Phi Phi

  • Tiger Cave Temple (Wat Tham Suea) — 1,237 steps to a golden Buddha panorama
  • Hong Island sea kayaking — paddle through caves into a hidden circular lagoon
  • Bor Thor mangrove kayaking (half-day, book separately or combined)
  • Afternoon ferry from Krabi Town pier to Koh Phi Phi Don (~2h — book ahead)
  • Check in Phi Phi — Tonsai Bay or Loh Dalum Bay for beach access
Eat: Krabi Town walking street lunch (Fri/Sat/Sun evening market near the waterfront). On Phi Phi: Papaya Restaurant on Tonsai Bay for Thai street food right off the boat.
09 Koh Phi Phi

Maya Bay at Dawn & Island Life

  • Long-tail boat to Maya Bay before 7 AM — golden light, far fewer crowds than midday
  • Snorkel at Bamboo Island and Shark Point — excellent reef fish, occasional reef sharks
  • Phi Phi Viewpoint hike — 30-min steep climb, 270-degree island panorama at the top
  • Loh Dalum Bay afternoon: kayaks, hammocks, and cold coconuts
  • Fire show on Tonsai Bay beach after dinner — nightly, free to watch
Eat: Papaya Thai for pad kra pao (best on the island). Grilled whole fish from any Loh Dalum Bay beach vendor at night. Mango sticky rice at the market stall near the main pier.
10 Phi Phi / Depart

Last Sunrise & Ferry to Phuket Airport

  • Sunrise kayak or paddleboard around the bay — the island is magical before 7 AM
  • Last bowl of Tom Kha Gai at any Tonsai restaurant before the ferry
  • Ferry Phi Phi → Phuket Rassada Pier (~2h — book the day before)
  • Grab or taxi from Rassada Pier to Phuket International Airport (HKT, ~30 min)
  • Fly home from Phuket
Eat: Airport note: Phuket HKT has decent Thai food in the international terminal — better than most regional airports. One last tom kha on the island is worth getting up early for.

Essential Dishes

Bangkok
Pad Thai
Done right: thin rice noodles wok-fried with egg, dried shrimp, bean sprouts, and fresh prawns, finished with lime, sugar, and crushed peanuts. The version at Thip Samai wraps it in a paper-thin egg crepe — a 1966 original.
→ Pad Thai Thip Samai (Mahachai Rd) · Jay Fai (Michelin)
Tom Yum Goong
Hot and sour prawn soup: lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaf, chilli, and plump river prawns in a clear broth. The clear version (nam sai) is more refined than the creamy red. Eat it everywhere — it varies enormously by cook.
→ Baan Klang Nam (riverside) · any local restaurant
Som Tam
Green papaya salad pounded in a mortar with fish sauce, lime, palm sugar, dried shrimp, and bird’s eye chilli. Specify "mai pet" unless you can handle real Thai heat. Always eaten with sticky rice and grilled chicken (kai yang).
→ Som Tam Nua (Siam Square) · Somtum Der
Pad Kra Pao
Holy basil stir-fry with pork mince (or chicken) and fresh chilli. Thailand’s real national dish — what Thais actually eat for lunch every day. Served with a fried egg on top over rice. Order with fresh chilli (prik sot) and specify your heat level.
→ Literally everywhere — this is what Thais eat daily
Chiang Mai
Khao Soi
Chiang Mai’s signature dish: rich coconut curry broth with egg noodles, crispy fried noodles on top, chicken or beef, pickled mustard greens, and shallots. A northern Thai dish influenced by Yunnan Chinese cuisine. Nothing else like it in Thailand.
→ Huen Phen · Khao Soi Lung Prakit Kad Kom · Khao Soi Mae Sai
Sai Oua (Northern Sausage)
Chiang Mai’s herbal pork sausage packed with lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime, and dried chilli. Grilled over charcoal and sold at every market in northern Thailand. Very different from any sausage you will eat elsewhere — fragrant, earthy, complex.
→ Warorot Market (Kad Luang) · Somphet Market
Gaeng Hang Lay
Northern Thai pork belly curry with ginger, turmeric, tamarind, and Shan spice paste. Dark, intense, and slightly sweet — nothing like the coconut-based curries of the south. Eaten at room temperature over sticky rice. Found at markets and local restaurants.
→ Huen Phen back dining room · local morning market stalls
Mango Sticky Rice
Glutinous sticky rice cooked in sweetened coconut milk, served with ripe champagne mango and a drizzle of thick coconut cream. Thailand’s most beloved dessert. Best when mango is in peak season (April–June). Available year-round but the quality swings significantly.
→ Mae Varee (Bangkok) · market vendors everywhere in season
Islands — Krabi & Phi Phi
Grilled Whole Fish
The entire southern island seafood experience centres on whole fish — sea bass, red snapper, or grouper stuffed with lemongrass and lime leaf, grilled over charcoal, served with a ferocious seafood dipping sauce. Order by weight at any beach shack.
→ Beach BBQ vendors on Loh Dalum Bay · Railay beach shacks
Tom Kha Gai
Coconut milk soup with galangal, lemongrass, and chicken — gentler and more fragrant than tom yum. The definitive island comfort food. Order it on every island and compare: the quality of the galangal-to-lime ratio changes everything.
→ Any Thai restaurant on Phi Phi, Railay, or Ao Nang
Massaman Curry
The south’s great slow-cooked curry — a Persian-influenced Muslim dish with beef or chicken, potatoes, peanuts, and warm spices (cinnamon, cardamom). Much milder and richer than Thai red or green curry. Once voted the world’s best food by CNN Travel.
→ Muslim restaurants in Krabi Town · Ao Nang halal food stalls
Roti with Banana & Condensed Milk
The southern Thai street dessert: a flaky fried flatbread (influenced by roti canai) cooked on a hot griddle, stuffed with banana, drizzled with condensed milk and chocolate sauce. Sold from night carts everywhere on the islands. Dangerous at midnight.
→ Any roti cart on Phi Phi, Ao Nang, or Patong

When to Visit

Peak Season
Cool & Dry
November — February
The definitive best time for all three regions simultaneously. Bangkok is pleasantly warm (25–30°C). Chiang Mai is genuinely cool, sometimes cold at night in December and January (pack a jacket). The Andaman islands are virtually rain-free.
  • Yi Peng sky lanterns in Chiang Mai (November)
  • Best sea conditions for Phi Phi and Krabi
  • Chiang Mai: bring a jacket for evenings Dec–Jan
  • Peak prices — book islands 3–6 months ahead
Good Value
Hot Season
March — May
Shoulder season: very hot and dry in Bangkok and the north (35–40°C in April — extreme). Songkran water festival in mid-April is a uniquely chaotic and joyful experience. The Andaman islands remain excellent through April.
  • Songkran Thai New Year (13–15 April) — water fights nationwide
  • Lower prices than Nov–Feb peak
  • Bangkok is very hot in April — plan outdoor time carefully
  • Islands excellent through April, transitioning in May
Islands Risk
SW Monsoon (Andaman)
May — October
The Andaman Coast (Krabi, Phi Phi, Phuket) receives heavy monsoon rain and rough seas May–October. Ferries occasionally cancel. Bangkok and Chiang Mai are fine — if visiting during this window, replace the island leg with Koh Samui (Gulf Coast, opposite monsoon cycle).
  • Krabi and Phi Phi risky May–Oct
  • Bangkok and Chiang Mai still excellent
  • Off-season deals: 30–50% cheaper accommodation
  • Gulf Coast (Koh Samui) is the island alternative
North Only
Post-Monsoon North
October — November
Chiang Mai and northern Thailand are stunning in October–November after monsoon ends: lush green landscapes, waterfalls at full flow, cool mountain air. The Andaman starts drying out by November. A great transitional window for this exact itinerary.
  • Chiang Mai at its most lush and green
  • Yi Peng Lantern Festival in November
  • Islands transitioning into dry season by late November
  • Bangkok comfortable and less humid

Critical: Thailand has two distinct monsoon cycles. The Andaman Coast (Krabi, Phi Phi, Phuket) is affected by the southwest monsoon from May–October. The Gulf Coast (Koh Samui, Koh Phangan) has the opposite pattern — its rainy season is October–December. This itinerary uses the Andaman — always plan island dates for November through April.

Transit & IC Cards

Between Cities
✈️
Domestic Flights (AirAsia / Nok Air / Thai Lion)
Essential for this itinerary — Bangkok to Chiang Mai (1h 20m) and Chiang Mai to Krabi (1h 40m) make flying the only practical option. AirAsia operates from Don Mueang Airport (DMK), which has better budget airline connections than Suvarnabhumi (BKK). Book 2–4 weeks ahead for good fares — last-minute prices spike sharply.
BKK→CNX: ~THB 900–2,200 · CNX→KBV: ~THB 1,200–3,000 · airasia.com
⛴️
Ao Nang / Krabi → Koh Phi Phi (Ferry)
Ferry from Krabi Town’s Klong Jilad Pier (or Ao Nang beach pier) to Phi Phi Don takes approximately 2 hours. Kok Chang Ferry and Tiger Ferry are the main operators. Book through your hotel or at the pier. Check weather and sea conditions before booking — operates reliably November through April.
~THB 350–450 · 2h crossing · Reliable Nov–Apr only
⛴️
Phi Phi → Phuket (Ferry) + HKT Airport
Ferry from Phi Phi to Phuket’s Rassada Pier takes 2 hours. Then Grab or taxi to Phuket International Airport (HKT) — approximately 30 minutes from the pier. Book the ferry the day before departure. Allows a morning on the island before the afternoon flight home — time it carefully.
Ferry: ~THB 250–350 · Taxi Rassada Pier→HKT: ~THB 350–450
City Transit & IC Cards
💳
Rabbit Card — Bangkok BTS Skytrain
The BTS Skytrain (above-ground) uses the Rabbit Card — a stored-value card that also works at Tops Supermarket, Starbucks, and many convenience stores. Buy at any BTS station ticket window for THB 100 (THB 30 deposit + THB 50 credit + THB 20 admin). Top up at ticket machines. Covers the main tourist zones: Silom, Sukhumvit, Siam, and the airport link interchange.
BTS Rabbit Card: THB 100 at any station · Fare per trip: THB 16–59
🚇
MRT Card — Bangkok Subway
The MRT underground uses a separate card from the BTS. Stored-value MRT cards work on the Blue Line and Purple Line. For a short visit, single-trip tokens from machines are easier. Key MRT stops: Chatuchak Park (weekend market), Sukhumvit (BTS interchange), Hua Lamphong (train station), Sam Yan (near Chinatown). The BTS and MRT systems are not interoperable.
Single trip token: THB 17–42 · MRT card available at all stations
🚗
Grab + Bolt — Bangkok & Chiang Mai
Both Grab and Bolt operate in Bangkok — Bolt is often 20–30% cheaper for short city trips. In Chiang Mai, Grab dominates (traditional tuk-tuks quote inflated tourist prices — always use Grab instead). On the islands, there’s no ride-hailing — negotiate long-tail boat fares directly or hire motorbike taxis (wear the helmet they hand you).
Bangkok short trip (Grab): ~THB 60–150 · Chiang Mai: ~THB 60–100

Essential Chains & Shopping

🏪 Convenience & Food Chains
🏪
7-Eleven Thailand
~14,000 locations · 24 hours · genuinely excellent
Thailand has more 7-Elevens per capita than almost anywhere, and they are genuinely good. The hot counter (look for the orange heat lamp) has freshly fried chicken, pork skewers, and fish balls for THB 15–25 each. Fresh rice sandwiches, steamed buns, and cold Chang beer are staples. The Meiji ice cream bars and banana milk are cult items among regulars. ATMs inside accept all cards.
Hot counter items at THB 20 each are the best value snack in Bangkok. Ministop (a rival chain) has better fried chicken and excellent soft-serve.
☕
Café Amazon
~4,500 locations · Thailand’s biggest café chain
Thailand’s PTT petrol station café chain has become the most ubiquitous coffee stop in the country — found at every highway rest stop, airport, shopping mall, and many standalone roadside locations. The Thai iced tea latte and coconut coffee are genuinely excellent at THB 55–75. Clean A/C seating, reliable WiFi, and consistent quality make it a lifesaver in the heat between sightseeing.
Order the Butterfly Pea Lemonade — vibrant blue-purple colour, refreshing, and photogenic. Also excellent: the Thai Tea Latte Frappe.
🛒
Lotus’s (Tesco) & Big C
Major hypermarkets · everywhere outside islands
Lotus’s (formerly Tesco Lotus) and Big C are Thailand’s dominant hypermarket chains — massive, fully air-conditioned, and carrying everything from excellent Thai prepared snacks to pharmacies and cheap clothing. The food halls inside are great value for snacks and drinks. Also the best place to buy cheap sunscreen, toiletries, and Mama instant noodles for the islands.
Buy Mama instant noodles in bulk (THB 6 each), Thai seasoning sachets, and snack mixes here — pack them for island accommodation where food options are limited and pricey.
🛍️ Markets & Shopping
🏪
Chatuchak Weekend Market — Bangkok
Saturday & Sunday · 8 AM–6 PM · 15,000+ stalls
One of the world’s great markets — 35 acres, 15,000 stalls, and organised into sections: plants, antiques, vintage clothing, ceramics, art, pets, and food. Get the official map at the entrance. Section 26–27: ceramics and Thai stoneware. Section 18: vintage and mid-century clothing. The food stalls inside Section 27 serve excellent and cheap Thai food. Arrive before 10 AM to avoid the heat and crowds.
Bring cash (THB). ATMs are scarce inside. Section 26: ceramics. Section 18: vintage. Food: go to Section 27 for the best stalls inside the market.
🌕
Chiang Mai Walking Streets
Saturday: Wualai Rd · Sunday: Tha Phae Gate · 4 PM–midnight
Chiang Mai’s walking markets are the best place to buy northern Thai handicrafts: hand-painted parasols, hill tribe textiles, silver jewellery, carved teak goods, and indigo-dyed cotton. The Sunday market on Wichayanon Street (north from Tha Phae Gate) is the larger and more artisanal of the two. The Warorot covered market nearby is the local daily market — best for sai oua sausage and local produce.
Best buys: hand-painted silk parasols (THB 200–400), hill tribe shoulder bags, lacquerware bowls, and northern Thai weaving. All significantly cheaper than Bangkok airport.
🌊
Island Shopping — Krabi & Phi Phi
Beach markets · Ao Nang walking street · Tonsai pier shops
The Ao Nang beachfront walking street has a cluster of souvenir shops, tailor stalls, and dive equipment stores. Phi Phi’s Tonsai Bay has a tight grid of shops selling the same sarongs, elephant print trousers, and hand-painted magnets everywhere in Thailand. Buy beach essentials (rash guards, waterproof bags) here — everything is modestly priced and readily available.
Best island buys: reef-safe sunscreen (cheaper here than at home), waterproof dry bags for kayaking, carved wooden fridge magnets, and woven palm leaf hats.

    Insider Tips

    🚲
    Book Elephant Nature Park Weeks Ahead

    ENP is one of the world’s most reputable ethical elephant sanctuaries and it books out weeks in advance during peak season (Nov–Feb). Go to elephantnaturepark.org directly — do not book through a third party. Full-day visit (~$80) includes transport, meals, and a guide. Never visit any sanctuary that offers elephant riding.

    🌸
    Temple Dress Code Is Non-Negotiable

    Shoulders and knees must be covered at every temple in Thailand — including the Grand Palace, Doi Suthep, and all wats in Chiang Mai. Many provide free sarongs at the entrance, but a lightweight scarf or long trousers packed for the day is cooler and faster. Grand Palace staff will turn you away at the gate.

    🌊
    Maya Bay: Dawn or Nothing

    Maya Bay was closed for three years to allow coral recovery and now admits a maximum daily visitor quota. Long-tail boats to Maya Bay leave Phi Phi’s Tonsai Pier from 6 AM. Arrive before 7:30 AM for the golden light and dramatically fewer people. By 10 AM the quota fills and the experience degrades significantly. Book your longtail the evening before.

    💵
    Cash, Taxi Meters & Tipping

    Thailand is largely cash-based outside hotels. Use Grab in cities to avoid meter disputes, but for Bangkok taxis always insist on the meter — say "meter khap/ka" firmly. Tipping: 20–40 THB at restaurants is generous, 100 THB at spa treatments. Carry THB 500–1,000 at all times; islands have limited ATMs and cards are often refused at small beach stalls.

    🌴
    Don Mueang (DMK) vs Suvarnabhumi (BKK)

    Bangkok has two airports. Suvarnabhumi (BKK) is the main international gateway. Don Mueang (DMK) handles most budget domestic airlines (AirAsia, Nok Air, Thai Lion). Check which airport your domestic flights use and allow 1.5–2h transit between the two by taxi or Grab if you’re connecting on the same trip. They are 30 km apart.

    🙏
    Cultural Respect in the Kingdom

    Never point your feet at a Buddha image or at a person — feet are considered the lowest, least sacred part of the body. Do not touch a monk’s robes (women especially). The King and royal family are deeply revered — any criticism is illegal and deeply offensive. Remove shoes when entering any home, temple, or traditional business.
