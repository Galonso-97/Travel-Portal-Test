The Route

Six cities, one arc

The itinerary traces a deliberate arc: north to centre, deep inland to the karst south, then east to the coast. Every transit is either a scenic high-speed train or a short flight — no overnight buses.

三
Day-by-Day Itinerary

Fourteen days in the Middle Kingdom

Each day is planned to be immersive but never frantic. Morning light almost always rewards early risers — especially at the Great Wall, on the Li River, and inside the Forbidden City before the crowds arrive.

I

Beijing 北京

Days 1 – 3  ·  Imperial China at its grandest

01Day
Beijing
Arrival, Tiananmen Square & the Hutongs

Land at Beijing Capital Airport and check in near the Dongcheng district — ideally a courtyard hotel inside a hutong alley. In the afternoon, walk the length of Tiananmen Square and stay for the flag-lowering ceremony at sunset, a deeply ceremonial ritual timed exactly to dusk. Afterwards, duck into the hutong maze around Nanluoguxiang: browse indie shops, drink craft beer at a hole-in-the-wall bar, and eat street skewers. If you have energy, catch a traditional Chinese opera performance at the nearby Liyuan Theatre — it begins at 19:30.

Tiananmen Square Flag-lowering ceremony at dusk Nanluoguxiang hutong alley Liyuan Theatre — Beijing opera Hutong street skewers (chuanr) Flag ceremony time changes daily with sunset — check online Stay within 2km of the Drum Tower for easy access
02Day
Beijing
Forbidden City, Jingshan Hill & Temple of Heaven

Queue at the Forbidden City when gates open at 8:30am — book tickets online 3–5 days ahead or they sell out. Spend three to four hours inside the 180-acre complex, ending at the Imperial Garden. Exit north through the back gate and immediately climb Jingshan Hill for the finest aerial view of the palace's golden rooftops — it's only 15 minutes up. Descend for lunch in the hutongs nearby, then taxi south to the Temple of Heaven where locals practise tai chi, ballroom dancing, and Beijing opera in the sweeping surrounding park. End the day with Peking duck at Da Dong or Quanjude. After dinner, stroll the lit-up Qianmen pedestrian street.

Forbidden City (9,999 rooms) Jingshan Hill — rooftop panorama Temple of Heaven Qianmen pedestrian street at night Peking duck — Da Dong or Quanjude Jianbing crêpe for breakfast (¥10) Pre-book Forbidden City tickets — they sell out 3+ days ahead The Imperial Garden at the north end is often overlooked — don't miss it
03Day
Beijing → Xi'an
Summer Palace, Great Wall & high-speed south

Start early at the Summer Palace — the vast imperial garden built around Kunming Lake is extraordinary in morning light, particularly the Long Corridor with its 14,000 painted scenes. Leave by 9am to beat crowds. Then take a bus or taxi to Mutianyu (1.5 hrs) — the least crowded restored Great Wall section, flanked by dense forest and equipped with a cable car up and a toboggan slide down. Aim for the wall by 11am. Return to Beijing by early afternoon, grab a bowl of zhajiangmian in Sanlitun or Gulou, then catch a G-train to Xi'an (4.5 hrs, arriving evening). Check in near the Bell Tower.

Summer Palace & Kunming Lake Long Corridor (14,000 painted panels) Mutianyu Great Wall Wall toboggan slide (¥55) Zhajiangmian lunch before departure Summer Palace: arrive before 8am for empty paths Mutianyu: sections 14–23 are least crowded and most dramatic G-trains Beijing West → Xi'an North: book 2 weeks ahead
II

Xi'an 西安

Days 4 – 5  ·  The ancient Silk Road capital

04Day
Xi'an
Terracotta Army, Huaqing Hot Springs & Muslim Quarter

Take the metro or tourist bus (40 mins) to the Terracotta Army early — Pit 1's scale hits hardest when it isn't packed with tour groups. All three pits hold over 8,000 unique figures; none of the faces are repeated. On the way back, stop at Huaqing Hot Springs — the Tang-dynasty imperial retreat where Emperor Xuanzong met Yang Guifei, China's most celebrated romance. Return to Xi'an by mid-afternoon. Spend the rest of the day inside the Muslim Quarter: wander the Great Mosque (one of China's oldest, built in 742 AD), browse Beiyuanmen street for stalls selling every Hui snack imaginable, and graze dinner over roujiamo, paomo soup, and lamb skewers. End with pomegranate juice — Xi'an's famous street drink.

Terracotta Army — all 3 pits Huaqing Hot Springs palace Great Mosque of Xi'an (742 AD) Beiyuanmen food street Roujiamo — slow-braised pork flatbread Yangrou paomo — lamb broth with torn bread Pomegranate juice (fresh-pressed stalls) Book Terracotta Army audio guide — worth every yuan Arrive at 8am: half the crowds, same warriors
05Day
Xi'an → Zhangjiajie
City Walls, Bell Tower, Little Goose Pagoda & fly south

Rent a bicycle (¥45/hr) at the South Gate and cycle the full 14km circuit of Xi'an's Ming-dynasty city walls at dawn — the stone battlements glow amber in early light and the city below is still quiet. Finish by 8:30am, return the bike, and walk five minutes to the Bell Tower for morning bells (rung at 9am and 11am). Head west to the Little Goose Pagoda, a delicate 45-metre 8th-century Buddhist tower set in a tranquil park — the museum inside is surprisingly rich. Grab a bowl of biangbiang noodles nearby before checking out. Fly to Zhangjiajie (1.5 hrs), arriving early evening. The landscape change from the yellow plains to steep forested gorges is visible even from the plane window.

Xi'an City Walls by bicycle Bell Tower (morning bells at 9am) Little Goose Pagoda & museum Shaanxi History Museum (free, book ahead) Biangbiang noodles — wide hand-pulled belts Persimmon cake at the south gate market City wall bike circuit: 2 hrs, best before 8am Shaanxi History Museum requires advance reservation — free entry
III

Zhangjiajie 张家界

Days 6 – 7  ·  The floating mountains of Avatar

06Day
Zhangjiajie
Tianmen Mountain, Glass Walkway & Avenue of Stars

Take the Tianmen Mountain cable car — the world's longest passenger ropeway at 7.5km — rising through cloud layers and past cliff faces to the summit plateau. Walk the west clifftop glass-bottomed walkway (1,400m altitude, 1.6m wide — not for the faint of heart). Continue to Tianmen Cave, a natural arch 131m high with a staircase of 999 steps climbing through it. On the way back down, the 99-switchback road through the canyon is one of China's most dramatic drives. In the evening, walk the lit Zhangjiajie City pedestrian Avenue of Stars — lined with national park-themed street art and lively food stalls.

Tianmen Mountain cable car (7.5km) West clifftop glass walkway Tianmen Cave — 999-step staircase 99-switchback mountain road Avenue of Stars evening stroll Tujia smoked pork with mountain peppers Start at 8am — cable car queues double by 10am Bring a light jacket — summit is 5°C cooler than the city
07Day
Zhangjiajie → Chongqing
Avatar Pillars, Bailong Elevator, Baofeng Lake & Golden Whip Stream

Enter Zhangjiajie National Forest Park at 7:30am on the 2-day pass. Ride the Bailong Elevator (the world's tallest outdoor lift at 326m, built into the cliff face) to the Yuanjiajie plateau — this is where the "Avatar Hallelujah Mountain" photo was taken, and the pillar formations stretch as far as you can see. Hike the Yuanjiajie Loop (2km, easy), then take the monorail across to the Tianzi Mountain area for wider views and fewer people. Descend in the afternoon and follow the Golden Whip Stream trail (5.7km, flat) — a riverside walk between the base of the pillars where macaque monkeys occasionally appear trailside. End at Baofeng Lake for a final boat ride through the gorge at dusk. Catch an evening flight or early-morning train to Chongqing.

Bailong Elevator (326m cliff lift) Yuanjiajie — Avatar Hallelujah Mountain Tianzi Mountain viewpoints Golden Whip Stream trail (5.7km) Baofeng Lake gorge boat ride 2-day park pass (¥248) covers all areas including elevator Yuanjiajie is most dramatic in early morning mist Golden Whip Stream: easy flat walk, allow 2 hours
IV

Chongqing 重庆

Days 8 – 9  ·  The mountain city of fire and fog

08Day
Chongqing
Ciqikou, Hongya Cave, Jiefangbei & the Yangtze at Night

Chongqing is unlike any other Chinese city — built vertically up forested ridges where two great rivers meet, with subway trains that emerge from cliff tunnels mid-station and skyscrapers stacked on hillsides. Start the morning in Ciqikou, a 1,700-year-old stone-paved river town with tea houses, craft stalls, and incredible street food. Walk east along the Jialing riverbank to Hongya Cave — an 11-storey stilted building cantilevered over the river gorge, packed with restaurants and bars. In the afternoon, explore the surreal Jiefangbei CBD, then take a 1-hour Yangtze River night cruise for the most dramatic skyline view in China. End the day at a proper Chongqing hotpot restaurant — order the split pot if you need mercy from the mala broth.

Ciqikou ancient river town Hongya Cave stilted riverside complex Jiefangbei CBD & Liberation Monument Yangtze River night cruise Eling Park — hilltop panorama of both rivers Chongqing hotpot (split pot recommended) Ciqikou pork rice — the town's famous dish Xiaomian noodles for breakfast (¥10) Chongqing in summer is notoriously humid — visit in spring or autumn Hongya Cave is best seen from the river at night
09Day
Chongqing → Guilin
Dazu Rock Carvings, Shibati Stairs & south to Guilin

Leave early for the UNESCO-listed Dazu Rock Carvings (2 hrs from Chongqing by hired car or tour) — over 50,000 cliff-carved Buddhist and Confucian figures dating from the Tang and Song dynasties, the finest religious rock art in China. The Beishan and Baoding Hill sites are the most impressive; allow 2.5 hours. Return to Chongqing and, before heading to the airport, climb the 18 Terraces (Shibati) — a dramatic stepped urban canyon of old staircase streets connecting the upper city to the river, recently restored and now lined with galleries and boutiques. This is old Chongqing at its most photogenic. Fly or take the high-speed train to Guilin (arriving evening), where the silhouettes of karst peaks against the lit sky announce you've entered a different world entirely.

Dazu Rock Carvings (UNESCO) — Baoding Hill Dazu Rock Carvings — Beishan Hill Shibati — 18 Terraces staircase district Liziba monorail station (train through building) Chuan Chuan skewers near Shibati Dazu: hire a driver rather than joining a tour — more flexible timing Liziba station: Line 2 train passes directly through a residential tower — surreal to witness
V

Guilin & Yangshuo 桂林 阳朔

Days 10 – 11  ·  The Li River & karst countryside

10Day
Guilin
Longji Rice Terraces, Elephant Trunk Hill & Night Lakes Cruise

Leave Guilin by 7am for the Longji Terraced Fields (2.5 hrs north) — Dragon's Backbone rice terraces carved into the mountainside by the Zhuang and Yao minorities over 600 years. Reach the No. 1 viewpoint before 9am for mist-wrapped morning light. Descend through Ping'an village and browse the minority craft stalls. Back in Guilin by early afternoon, visit Elephant Trunk Hill — the city's emblem, a natural limestone arch that looks exactly like a drinking elephant. Walk the riverside park around it and explore the small cave beneath. Then head to Seven Star Park, Guilin's largest, where karst peaks rise directly from its lawns and caves run beneath them. Evening: the Two Rivers and Four Lakes illuminated boat tour circuits Guilin's central canal system past pagodas and lit bridges — genuinely one of the most beautiful night tours in China.

Longji Rice Terraces — No. 1 viewpoint Ping'an minority village Elephant Trunk Hill & cave Seven Star Park & caves Two Rivers Four Lakes night boat tour Guilin rice noodles for breakfast (¥8) Stuffed Li River snails at a night stall Best terraces: Oct–Nov (golden harvest) or June (vivid green) Book the night boat tour at your hotel — fills up fast
11Day
Guilin → Yangshuo → Shanghai
Li River cruise, Moon Hill, Yulong River & fly to Shanghai

The Li River cruise from Guilin to Yangshuo (4 hrs) passes the exact scenery printed on China's 20-yuan note — a procession of reflected karst peaks, bamboo groves, and fishing boats at the bends. Take a shared bamboo raft rather than the tourist boat for a quieter, lower perspective. Arrive in Yangshuo and rent an e-bike or bicycle: ride the Yulong River greenway south (10km, flat) past stone bridges and water buffalo to the ancient Fuli village. Return for lunch and a plate of beer fish. In the afternoon, hike to Moon Hill — a 20-minute scramble up to a limestone arch with a 360° karst panorama. Head back to Guilin for an evening flight or train to Shanghai, arriving late. Tomorrow the city will be a complete shock to the system.

Li River cruise — 20-yuan note scenery Yulong River greenway cycling (10km) Fuli ancient village Moon Hill limestone arch hike West Street — Yangshuo's lively night market Beer fish — Yangshuo's signature dish Yangshuo farmhouse lunch along the Yulong Bamboo raft: ¥200–300, 4 hrs — far more atmospheric than tourist boat Moon Hill: go late afternoon for golden light through the arch
VI

Shanghai 上海

Days 12 – 14  ·  The city that lives in the future

12Day
Shanghai
The Bund, French Concession, Tianzifang & Xintiandi

Walk the Bund at 6:30am before tour groups arrive — the kilometre-long promenade of neoclassical European bank buildings facing Pudong's sci-fi skyline across the Huangpu is Shanghai at its most cinematic. Cross to Pudong on the tourist ferry for the opposite angle. Back in Puxi, walk south through the French Concession: tree-lined Wukang Road is the most photogenic street in Shanghai, lined with art deco villas, independent cafes, and boutiques. Browse the labyrinthine Tianzifang arts and craft lanes, then walk north to Xintiandi — a beautifully restored shikumen longtang neighbourhood turned upmarket dining district. Lunch of xiaolongbao at Jia Jia Tang Bao or the original Din Tai Fung. End the day watching Pudong light up from a rooftop bar on the Bund.

The Bund at sunrise Bund tourist ferry to Pudong Wukang Road — art deco villas Tianzifang arts lanes Xintiandi shikumen district Rooftop bar on the Bund at dusk Xiaolongbao — Jia Jia Tang Bao (queue outside) Shengjianbao pan-fried buns from a street cart Wukang Road: best photographed at golden hour Jia Jia Tang Bao: cash only, arrive before 11am to avoid a long queue
13Day
Shanghai
Yu Garden, Old Town, Shanghai Tower & Pudong skyline

Spend the morning in the Yu Garden complex — the classical Ming-dynasty garden of rockeries, pavilions, and koi ponds is surrounded by a bazaar of tea houses, silk shops, and snack stalls. Don't miss the Huxinting Tea House, the most famous in Shanghai, sitting on a zigzag bridge over the central pond. Explore the nearby City God Temple and the old town alleyways (Laocheng district) — this is the quietest remnant of pre-colonial Shanghai. After lunch, cross to Pudong: the Shanghai Tower observation deck at 546m (highest in China, ¥220) delivers a vertiginous, strangely humbling view. Walk to the neighbouring SWFC Sky Walk for a second angle. In the evening, walk the Lujiazui waterfront and take in the light show that begins at 20:00.

Yu Garden — Ming-dynasty classical garden Huxinting Tea House on the zigzag bridge City God Temple & Laocheng old town Shanghai Tower observation deck (546m) SWFC Sky Walk (bottle-opener building) Lujiazui waterfront light show (20:00) Crab soup dumplings at the Yu Garden bazaar Hairy crab (Oct–Nov season only) Shanghai Tower: book tickets online to skip the queue Yu Garden: arrive before 9am — it becomes extremely crowded by midday
14Day
Shanghai
West Bund, M50 Art District, Jing'an Temple & departure

The West Bund waterfront district is Shanghai at its most forward-looking — the Long Museum, Tank Shanghai, and Yuz Museum sit in former industrial buildings along the river, housing world-class contemporary art collections. Walk north along the riverfront to the M50 Creative Park in Suzhou Creek — a network of former textile factories converted into artist studios and galleries, many of them free to enter and browse. By midday, visit Jing'an Temple: a Buddhist temple of extraordinary gold and lacquer grandeur, improbably surrounded by luxury skyscrapers in central Shanghai, functioning as an active place of worship even today. Grab a final meal in Jing'an's side streets before catching the Maglev (430 km/h, ¥50, 8 minutes) from Pudong Airport — the fastest way to leave a city on earth.

West Bund arts waterfront Long Museum & Tank Shanghai M50 Creative Park — free artist studios Jing'an Temple — gold Buddhist temple Maglev train to airport (430 km/h) Brunch in Jing'an — Shanghai's best café neighbourhood Red-braised pork for a final lunch Allow 3.5 hrs minimum to reach Pudong Airport M50: most studios open 10am–6pm, free entry — hidden gem
四
Culinary Guide

What to eat, city by city

Chinese cuisine is six cuisines arguing with each other. This itinerary covers four distinct culinary regions — each one a revelation. Never order from the tourist menu if you can gesture at what the table next to you is eating.

✦ ✦ ✦
Beijing
Peking Duck 北京烤鸭

Lacquered, slow-roasted whole duck sliced tableside and wrapped in thin pancakes with plum sauce. Da Dong and Quanjude are the historic institutions.

Beijing
Zhajiangmian

Thick wheat noodles topped with fermented soybean paste and shredded vegetables. A humble, intensely satisfying bowl found in any Beijing hutong.

Xi'an
Roujiamo 肉夹馍

Slow-braised spiced pork stuffed into a flatbread — legitimately the world's oldest sandwich. The Hui version uses mutton and cumin.

Xi'an
Biangbiang Noodles

Hand-pulled belts of wheat noodle, wide as a hand and slapped flat on the board. Topped with chilli oil, garlic, and vinegar. Written with the most complex Chinese character in existence.

Chongqing
Chongqing Hotpot 火锅

A roiling vat of mala (numbing-spicy) broth. Order the split pot if you need mercy. Tripe, lotus root, and fatty beef are essential. Drink cold Tsingtao between bites.

Chongqing
Xiaomian 小面

Chongqing's breakfast noodle — thin wheat noodles in a punishingly flavourful spicy broth, topped with peanuts, scallions, and chilli oil. ¥10 from any street stall.

Guilin
Guilin Rice Noodles 桂林米粉

Thin rice vermicelli in a slow-simmered pork and herb broth, topped with marinated meats and crispy fried peanuts. Eaten at breakfast by every local, every morning.

Yangshuo
Beer Fish 啤酒鱼

Freshwater fish from the Li River, braised in local Liquan beer with peppers, tomatoes, and spring onions. Every restaurant on West Street claims to have the original recipe.

Shanghai
Xiaolongbao 小笼包

Shanghai's transcendent soup dumpling — thin pork skin encasing a molten pork and ginger broth. Place in a soup spoon, bite a corner, sip the soup, then eat whole. Never rush this.

Shanghai
Shengjianbao 生煎包

Pan-fried cousin of the XLB — doughy, crispy-bottomed, generously filled. Found on street corners throughout the city for ¥8–12. Best with a cup of hot soy milk.

Shanghai
Hairy Crab 大闸蟹

In season (October–November): steamed hairy crab from Yangcheng Lake, served with black vinegar and ginger. A seasonal obsession that brings the city to its knees each autumn.

Everywhere
Street Breakfast 早餐

Every Chinese city has its own breakfast culture. Jianbing (savoury crêpes) in Beijing, warm soy milk and you tiao everywhere, egg-and-chive boxes in Xi'an. Never skip it.

Beijing
Jianbing 煎饼

Beijing's iconic breakfast crêpe — thin mung bean batter spread on a griddle, cracked with a fresh egg, smeared with fermented bean paste and chilli, wrapped around a crunchy wonton wafer. Eaten standing up. ¥8–12.

Xi'an
Yangrou Paomo 羊肉泡馍

A Hui Muslim classic: thick flatbread torn by hand into tiny pieces, then slow-simmered in rich mutton broth. The tearing is part of the ritual — the smaller the pieces, the better the bowl.

Chongqing
Chuan Chuan 串串香

Skewered meats and vegetables self-cooked in a communal mala broth — like hotpot on a stick. Order by the basket, count your sticks at the end. Popular late-night street snack.

Zhangjiajie
Tujia Smoked Pork 土家腊肉

Tujia minority smoked pork, cured over wood-fire for months and stir-fried with wild mountain peppers and garlic shoots. A deeply savoury, smoky dish unique to western Hunan.

Shanghai
Red-Braised Pork 红烧肉

Shanghai's soul dish — pork belly slow-braised in soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and rock sugar until lacquered and trembling. Every local grandmother has a version. None of them share the recipe.

Beijing
Douzhi 豆汁

Beijing's most divisive drink — a fermented mung bean liquid with a pungent, sour tang. Old-timers swear by it; most visitors recoil. Try it once at a hutong tea house. Respect is earned either way.

Xi'an
Persimmon Cake 柿子饼

Flat, chewy pastries made from Shaanxi persimmon pulp, filled with red bean or walnut and fried until golden. Sold by street vendors near the Bell Tower. Sticky, sweet, and impossible to eat neatly.

Guilin
Stuffed Li River Snails 螺蛳

Tiny river snails stuffed with pork, black bean, and chilli, then stewed in a clay pot. A late-night street snack eaten with toothpicks at plastic tables under fluorescent lights. Deeply local.

Getting Around

China's transport network

China has the world's largest high-speed rail network — over 40,000km of track. For journeys under four hours, trains beat flights on overall journey time and experience. Book through Trip.com or the 12306 app (use a VPN to access the English version).

🚄
High-Speed Rail
G & D Trains

Bullet trains run at 300–350 km/h. Perfectly punctual. The Beijing–Xi'an route (4.5 hrs) and Shanghai–Guilin route are stunning. Book 2 weeks ahead for popular routes.

✈️
Domestic Flights
Budget Airlines

Zhangjiajie–Chongqing and Guilin–Shanghai are best done by air. China Eastern, Air China, and Hainan Airlines are the main carriers. Trip.com aggregates all.

🚇
City Metro
Subway Systems

Beijing, Shanghai, and Chongqing have world-class metro systems. Use Alipay or WeChat Pay to tap in. A single trip rarely exceeds ¥8 (~€1).

🛺
City Taxis
DiDi App

DiDi is China's Uber — essential for anywhere the metro doesn't reach. Use DiDi Express. Have your hotel write your destination in Chinese characters for the driver.

🚌
Long-Distance
Tourist Shuttles

For Zhangjiajie park access and Li River cruises, book through your hotel or local agencies. Avoid private touts at train stations.

🛳️
River Boat
Li River Cruise

The official tourist boats are fine but expensive. A shared bamboo raft is cheaper and more atmospheric. Book through your Guilin hotel the night before.

🚡
Cable Cars
Mountain Ropeways

Tianmen Mountain's 7.5km ropeway is the world's longest passenger cable car. The Zhangjiajie Bailong Elevator (outdoor, 326m) is also unique. Both require separate tickets.

🚆
Overnight Train
Sleeper Services

China's overnight sleeper trains are surprisingly comfortable and excellent value. A soft-sleeper berth on the Chongqing–Guilin route saves a hotel night and arrives at dawn.

🛵
Bike & Scooter
Shared Cycling

Meituan and Hello Bike are everywhere in Beijing, Shanghai, and Guilin. Scan with Alipay and pay per minute. Yangshuo's countryside is best explored by rented bicycle or e-scooter.

🚐
Airport Links
Express Trains

Shanghai's Maglev (430 km/h, ¥50) and Beijing's Airport Express are both fast and stress-free. In Xi'an, bus line 43 connects the airport to the Bell Tower in under an hour for ¥20.

🗺️
Navigation
Maps & Booking

Baidu Maps works without a VPN and handles transit directions well. For train tickets, the Trip.com app (English) is the easiest option for foreigners — no need for the notoriously fiddly 12306 app.

What does two weeks cost?

China is remarkably affordable for a destination of its scale and quality. The biggest variable is accommodation — budget options are excellent (China's hostel scene is world-class) but mid-range hotels deliver exceptional value. Meals from street stalls and local restaurants cost a fraction of what you'd expect.

七
When to Go

Seasons across a continent

China's vast geography means seasons behave differently in each city on this itinerary. As a general rule: spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) are the consensus sweet spot — comfortable temperatures, manageable crowds.

Spring
March — May
★★★★★

Mild, clear, and green. Cherry blossom in Beijing (late March). Rice terraces lush and vivid. Crowds manageable before Golden Week. Ideal for this itinerary.

Summer
June — August
★★★★★

Hot and humid across the south. Zhangjiajie clouds add drama. Beijing hot but bearable. Shanghai is a furnace. July–August is peak domestic travel — book weeks ahead.

Autumn
Sept — November
★★★★★

The finest season. Golden rice terraces at Longji. Hairy crab in Shanghai. Crisp air in Beijing and Xi'an. Colours in the karst landscape are extraordinary in October.

Winter
Dec — February
★★★★★

Beijing and Xi'an are cold to bitter. The south (Guilin, Shanghai) stays mild. Crowds at their minimum. The Forbidden City in snow is an extraordinary sight, if you time it right.

Avoid: Chinese National Holiday Golden Week (Oct 1–7) and Spring Festival / Chinese New Year (late Jan to mid-Feb). Train tickets sell out weeks in advance and major sights hit capacity limits.

八
Practical Essentials

Before you go — the real list

China has a few unique logistical considerations that reward preparation. Sort these before you fly and the trip runs on rails (literally).

Visa

  • Most nationalities need a tourist visa (L visa)
  • Apply 2–4 weeks ahead at a Chinese embassy
  • 144-hour visa-free transit available in many cities for some nationalities — check eligibility
  • Passport valid 6+ months beyond travel dates

VPN & Internet

  • Google, WhatsApp, Instagram, and most Western apps are blocked
  • Download and test a VPN before you land (ExpressVPN, Astrill, or NordVPN work reliably)
  • Use WeChat for messaging locally
  • Baidu Maps works without VPN for navigation

Payments

  • Set up Alipay (International version now supports foreign cards)
  • Carry ¥500–1,000 in cash as backup
  • ICBC and Bank of China ATMs are safest for foreign cards
  • Most big hotels accept Visa/Mastercard

SIM Card

  • Buy a Chinese SIM on arrival (airport counters) — China Mobile, China Unicom, or China Telecom
  • Alternatively: get a Hong Kong SIM before arrival for broader app access
  • Register your passport at the carrier shop

Health & Safety

  • Tap water is not safe to drink — bottled water everywhere
  • Air quality: bring an N95 mask, especially Beijing
  • Travel insurance with medical cover is essential
  • No specific vaccines required; hepatitis A/B recommended

Key Apps

  • DiDi — taxis and ride-hailing
  • Trip.com — trains, flights, hotels
  • WeChat — messaging, some payments
  • Alipay — payments and QR codes everywhere
  • Baidu Translate — offline Chinese translation

Etiquette

  • Push and queue culture — relax your European reflexes
  • Loud conversation is normal in restaurants
  • Use both hands when exchanging business cards
  • Avoid discussing Taiwan, Tibet, or politics
  • Photography: always ask before photographing individuals

Useful Phrases

  • Nǐ hǎo (你好) — Hello
  • Xièxiè (谢谢) — Thank you
  • Duōshǎo qián? (多少钱?) — How much?
  • Wǒ bù chī ròu (我不吃肉) — I don't eat meat
